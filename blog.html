Structure for "Brown v. JFK"

1. Introduction

We meet our two main characters ; Elaine Brown and JFK. Elaine writes a letter to JFK (who is in his 2nd term due to him not having been assassinated) pleading with him to end the war in Vietnam and discussing her connection with the black panthers, making a case for their legitimacy as an institution. JFK is intrigued and invites her to the white house for a meeting

2. Rising action

Elaine prepares to meet JFK ; when they meet, there is immediate tension, as he is part of the “establishment’ that keeps Elaine & folks like her down. Things begin cordially but quickly elaine turns things political – jfk tries to smooth things over, dodging her inquiries and statements. She keeps at him. JFK is already on the fence about some of these things but his advisors and notably lyndon johnson are pressuring him to keep the war on.

3. Climax

– until he breaks. She accuses him of inviting her just to “look progressive” and tells the story of her friends who have died both in the war and domestically, appealing to his better judgement, finally

4. Falling action

Her wit having won him over, JFK stands up to LBJ to pull all the troops out of vietnam, and drafts his version of the civil rights act.

5. Denoument

We are shown a future where this conversation had been allowed to take place should JFK had not been assassinated – and the implications that it would lead to historic black leaders such as MLK and Malcolm X also not getting assassinated, a better world where nixon nor reagan would’ve been allowed into power :)


From User to Character

“Angela is described in such an anonymous way that it never becomes clear why she acts as she does. She could be anyone or actually no one.” (pg. 3)

This is an important piece of insight to keep in mind when crafting a character. Sometimes, in order to create a character the audience can relate to, writers will make someone who is too vague; but this will make the audience have the opposite reaction. Just because someone’s situation isn’t necessarily relatable, the emotion the person feels when going through a situation can be universal. Therefore it is important to give all characters a sense of personality and unique agency in order to make someone truly “relatable.”


“Film script writing has a lot of rules established taking into account the transformation from paper to screen. One such rule is only to describe what can be seen.” (pg. 4)

I’ve taken a few screenwriting courses and one of the first pieces of advice given is “show, don’t tell.” Relying too heavily on narration of events can seem like you are telling the audience how to feel. If you, instead, show them the event, you can shape their emotional reaction in a different way. It’s not just limited to screenwriting – print can also use the “show, don’t tell” rule by describing actions rather than emotions. For example, saying “She was scared of heights” is not quite as effective as “When she looked down, she became sick to her stomach.”


“To describe the user as a rounded character brings a focus on the user into the design process.” (pg. 6)

I appreciated this bit of insight since we are focusing on the “human” part of “human-computer interaction.” Everyone is a unique individual with their own sets of ideals, skills, and methodologies. Identifying a unique perspective and applying it to your designs can help a designer find specific problems that they might not otherwise find.

What If...?

Sept 15, 2022

Yamadonga on DS9

Star Trek is a great series because any and every crossover makes sense, including the one I’ve just considered which is Deep Space 9 and the 2007 Tollywood mass film “Yamadonga” (trans. “The Thief of Yama”).

We find Raja, the Thief of Yama himself, suddenly sucked through some kind of time wormhole from 21st century Hyderabad; he emerges on Deep Space 9 (the Federation-controlled space station between the Delta Wormhole and Bajor) in the 24th century! Keeping in mind that Raja has already died, gone to hell, taken over, and subsequently been brought back to life, we know that he is quick to adapt to new and strange situations. Perhaps he thinks he’s in Hell again, though it looks quite a bit different now. Classic fish-out-of-water antics ensue as the crew of DS9 tries to figure out how this 21st century trickster got here. He’s impressed by food replicators. He finds the futuristic fashions drab. Looking out at space is always a remarkable sight! Always a hustler, Raja would likely find his way to Quark’s Bar and start scamming the Dabo tables. Quark would notice and they would team up to rip off customers and gamblers, much to the disappointment of Odo (chief of security.)

He would do well at DS9 in general, befriending crew members and civilians (the ones he didn’t cheat out of their latinum) alike. There would be at least one chase/fight scene, maybe where he takes down a criminal of some kind to earn the trust of the DS9 crew; or maybe an acrobatic clash of titans between him and Major Kira. Since Raja’s been transported from a masala film there also would be a dance scene with very little context or relationship to the overarching story.

Eventually, the crew would solve the time crisis and find a way to get Raja back home to his new wife, with all the gold-pressed latinum he earned scamming at Quark’s.



What if...?


  • ...the black plague wiped out 90% of Europe (instead of the estimated 30-60%)?
  • ...Antonin Scalia had been assassinated in 1999?
  • ...JFK hadn’t been assassinated?

My alternate history where JFK was allowed to live would follow two different characters: JFK himself and a somewhat-fictionalized version of Elaine Brown (who, in real life, would be the chairwoman for the Black Panther party in the 70’s). While the struggle for Civil Rights and the Vietnam War raged, Elaine would appeal her grievances to JFK via letter; he would invite her of them to the White House where a stage-play type story would take place between the president and Elaine; where she would convince him to end the cold war and create more social justice reforms ~ countless lives saved and Nixon never getting elected ~

Storytelling in UX

Sept. 9, 2022

On Perspective
“You could use the perspective of the city itself [when telling a story], showing how all the time spent trying to park adds up across the population.” Pg. 334

When telling stories, we often get caught up in the point of view in the characters, so much so that we occasionally neglect other forms of perspective. I appreciated this example of a less conventional perspective of a city itself; many of my favorite stories treat the setting as a character in itself, whether it’s the cyberpunk atmosphere of Blade Runner (that tells a story about hypercapitalism / the effects of climate change) or the southern bayou of True Detective S1 (showing the results of a neglected community). In this example, using the city as an example of trying to reform car parking structure is great, when one considers all the physical space that parking garages/parking lots take up in a city VS. the amount of physical space taken up by public transit.


On Confessional Tales
“This might be a risky strategy. After all, if you are an expert, shouldn’t you be sharing that expertise, not your own problems and personal experiences?” Pg. 338

One of the most important factors of storytelling these days is ethos. It can be easy to separate yourself from the story you are trying to tell, but the best stories, in my opinion, portray the author in them in some way or another; whether it’s by describing something that actually happened to them or acknowledging through fiction something that affects them in their real life. It can be risky when trying to sell a product to showcase yourself as having a problem. But people are more affected if someone is telling a passionate story that happened to them vs. something that happened to a fictional character. It also presents an opportunity for the author or presenter to be humble and bring a humanistic touch to their work, which immediately can get the audience on your side.


On Historical Context
“Historical context can be a small or quick reference to something that the audience might recognize, either specifically (like the Vietnam War) or metaphorically: “Back when the world was young,” or, “Back when dialing a phone meant using an actual dial.” Pg. 360

Similarly to my thoughts on perspective, using the frame of historical context can be one of the things that makes a story the most effective. By using historical context you can quickly set the mood of a story. One of the examples above is the Vietnam War — when a story is set during this period, there is immediately a sense of urgency and drama created due to the understanding that not only the war was happing, but large cultural shifts as well from Civil Rights to the Space Race. This, vs. setting a story during the 00’s, where personal technology is extremely prevalent, changing the world completely with computers, cellphones, and the internet (it’s a lot harder to frame a horror movie where a protagonist can’t access the phone in an age where everyone has a phone on them, for example).
